# Sample Articles

Five ready-to-use examples for testing the content pipeline end-to-end.

---

## 1. CSRD Double Materiality for First-Timers

**What is it?**
A practical guide to conducting a double materiality assessment for companies approaching CSRD compliance for the first time

**Who is it for?**
Sustainability managers

**Key claims:**
- Only 15% of companies subject to CSRD wave 1 had a documented double materiality process before the deadline (source: EFRAG readiness survey 2024)
- Double materiality requires assessing both impact materiality (your effect on the world) and financial materiality (the world's effect on you) — separately, then together

**Our angle:**
Most companies treat double materiality as a box-ticking exercise. Done properly, it's the most strategic document a sustainability team can produce — it tells you where to focus for the next 3 years.

**Avoid:**
Don't conflate double materiality with the TCFD single materiality approach. Don't cite pre-2023 EFRAG guidance, it was superseded.

## 2. Scope 3 Emissions: What's Actually Required

**What is it?**
What Scope 3 reporting actually requires under ESRS E1 — cutting through the confusion on which categories apply and how to calculate them

**Who is it for?**
ESG analysts & reporting teams

**Key claims:**
- ESRS E1 requires disclosure of all material Scope 3 categories, not all 15 — but companies must justify why categories are immaterial
- Category 11 (use of sold products) is typically the largest Scope 3 source for manufacturing companies, often 70-80% of total footprint

**Our angle:**
The Scope 3 debate is mostly noise. The real question isn't "do we have to report it" — it's "which categories are material for us and how do we get credible data from our supply chain." Start there.

**Avoid:**
Don't frame this as optional. Don't use the GHG Protocol category names without mapping them to ESRS E1 equivalents — they don't align 1:1.

## 3. The CFO's Guide to CSRD Financial Risk

**What is it?**
How CFOs and finance teams should think about the financial materiality dimension of CSRD — and what it means for risk disclosure and investor reporting

**Who is it for?**
CFOs & finance leaders

**Key claims:**
- Under ESRS, financial materiality triggers are the same thresholds used in IFRS financial statements — this is intentional and connects sustainability to mainstream reporting
- 63% of institutional investors say they've adjusted valuations based on sustainability risk disclosures in the past 12 months (source: BlackRock 2024 stewardship report)

**Our angle:**
CSRD isn't a sustainability team problem — it's a CFO problem. The financial materiality assessment is effectively a new risk register, and finance teams who own it will be ahead of those who outsource it to ESG consultants.

**Avoid:**
Don't frame CSRD as a cost or compliance burden. The angle is opportunity and risk management, not overhead.

## 4. Supplier Engagement for Scope 3 Data

**What is it?**
How procurement and supply chain teams can build a supplier data collection process that actually works — without alienating key suppliers

**Who is it for?**
Procurement & supply chain managers

**Key claims:**
- The average enterprise has 1,200+ active suppliers — trying to collect emissions data from all of them is the wrong approach; tier by spend and materiality first
- CDP supply chain program participation increased 24% in 2024, but response rates average only 51% without active engagement

**Our angle:**
Supplier sustainability data collection fails because it's treated as a reporting project. It succeeds when procurement teams tie it to supplier relationship management and commercial incentives. The data quality follows the relationship quality.

**Avoid:**
Don't recommend survey-blasting all suppliers. Don't suggest spend thresholds without acknowledging sector variance (a €50k logistics supplier can have higher emissions relevance than a €500k software vendor).

## 5. What the ESRS Omnibus Simplification Actually Changes

**What is it?**
A clear breakdown of what the EU's ESRS Omnibus proposal changes for companies currently preparing for CSRD — what's deferred, what's dropped, what still applies

**Who is it for?**
Compliance officers

**Key claims:**
- The Omnibus proposal raises the CSRD threshold to 1,000+ employees, exempting ~80% of originally in-scope companies from wave 2 and 3
- Wave 1 companies (already reporting) are unaffected — their obligations remain unchanged
- The proposal is not yet law — it still requires EP and Council approval, expected Q3 2025 at earliest

**Our angle:**
The Omnibus is being misread in both directions. Some companies are using it as an excuse to pause all sustainability work — that's wrong. Others are panicking about scope changes that don't affect them. The right response is to understand exactly where you sit and adjust accordingly, not react to headlines.

**Avoid:**
Don't present the Omnibus as final or enacted — it's a proposal. Don't say companies are "off the hook" — even exempted companies face growing investor and customer pressure regardless of legal obligation.

## 6. How to Prepare Your Supply Chain for CBAM Reporting

**What is it?**
Practical steps for getting your suppliers ready to provide the emissions data you need for CBAM compliance, especially if you're importing from outside the EU.

**Who is it for?**
Procurement & supply chain managers

**Key claims:**
- CBAM requires importers to report embedded emissions for covered goods (steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, electricity, hydrogen). From 2026 you'll need actual supplier-level data, not just defaults.
- Most non-EU suppliers have never calculated product-level emissions before. If you wait until they're required to provide it, you'll be scrambling.

**Our angle:**
This isn't just a compliance exercise. Companies that start engaging suppliers on emissions data now will have better data quality, fewer surprises at the border, and stronger supplier relationships. Treat it as a procurement capability, not a one-off reporting task. Walk through a realistic timeline: what to do 12 months out, 6 months out, and at go-live.

**Avoid:**
Don't assume readers already understand embedded emissions calculations. Keep the technical stuff accessible. Don't focus only on the penalty risk, focus on the operational reality of actually collecting this data from suppliers who may not speak the same sustainability language.

# Coolset Academy Style Guide

> Extracted from analysis of ~100 published Coolset Academy articles. Use this guide to prompt LLMs for content generation that matches Coolset's voice.

---

## Voice & Tone

### Professional but approachable
Coolset writes like a knowledgeable colleague explaining regulations — authoritative without being academic, clear without being condescending. The tone sits between a consultancy whitepaper and a well-written blog post.

### Direct and practical
Every article is oriented toward action. The reader should finish knowing what to do, not just what something is. Sentences lead with the point, then provide context.

**Do:** "CBAM requires importers to pay for greenhouse gas emissions embedded in specific goods."
**Don't:** "It is important to understand that there are certain requirements that may apply to importers regarding emissions."

### Second-person address
Articles speak directly to the reader using "you" and "your." This creates a sense of guidance rather than lecturing.

**Do:** "If you import in-scope products into the EU, you'll need to register as an authorised CBAM declarant."
**Don't:** "Companies importing in-scope products into the EU are required to register."

### Confident but not salesy
Coolset states facts and positions clearly. There's no hedging language ("maybe," "perhaps," "it could be argued") unless genuine uncertainty exists (e.g., pending regulation). Product mentions are matter-of-fact, never pushy.

## Structure & Formatting

### Article anatomy
1. **Title** — Clear, keyword-rich, often includes the regulation name and a descriptor (e.g., "What is CBAM: The EU Importer's Guide to the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism")
2. **Key Takeaways** — 2–4 bullet points at the top summarising the article's core points
3. **Body** — Logical progression: What it is → Why it matters → How it works → Who's affected → What to do
4. **FAQ section** (optional) — Short Q&A pairs addressing common questions, useful for SEO

### Heading hierarchy
- **H2** for major sections (e.g., "How CBAM Works: Calculate, Report, Pay")
- **H3** for subsections within those sections
- Headings are descriptive and scannable — a reader skimming only headings should grasp the article's arc

### Paragraph length
Short paragraphs: 1–4 sentences. Dense walls of text are avoided. Each paragraph makes one point.

### Lists
- **Bullet lists** for 3+ non-sequential items (requirements, criteria, exemptions)
- **Numbered lists** for sequential processes or steps
- List items are concise — typically one sentence or a short phrase with a brief explanation

### Bold text
Used for key terms on first mention, emphasis on critical dates/thresholds, and labelling items within lists.

**Example:**
- **Impact Materiality (inside-out):** How your company's activities affect people and the environment
- **Financial Materiality (outside-in):** How sustainability issues affect financial performance

### Callout quotes
Important regulatory quotes or key statements are pulled out in quotation marks as standalone paragraphs for emphasis.

**Example:**
> "A qualitative analysis may be sufficient for the undertaking to reasonably conclude on materiality of impacts, risks or opportunities."

## Language & Conventions

### Spelling
British English for EU-specific terminology (e.g., "authorised," "organisation," "fertilisers"), but generally consistent within each article. Follow British English as the default.

### Acronyms
Spell out on first use with the acronym in parentheses, then use the acronym throughout.

**Example:** "Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)" → then "CSRD" for the rest of the article.

### Dates and numbers
- Full date format: "1 January 2026" (day month year, no ordinal suffixes)
- Percentages: use the % symbol with a number (e.g., "55%"), spell out in flowing prose if starting a sentence
- Currency: €100, €150 (symbol before number, no space)

### Regulation references
Always use the full name on first mention, then the abbreviation. Treat regulation names as proper nouns.

**Examples:** European Green Deal, Fit for 55, EU Emissions Trading Scheme (ETS), ESRS E1

### Contractions
Light use of contractions is acceptable — "you'll," "won't," "isn't," "it's." This keeps the tone conversational without being too informal. Avoid double contractions or slang.

## Content Principles

### Ground everything in specifics
Abstract claims are always backed by concrete details: specific dates, thresholds, regulation names, percentages. Coolset articles rarely make a point without evidence or a specific reference.

**Do:** "From 1 January 2026, importers must buy CBAM certificates to cover those emissions."
**Don't:** "Soon, importers will need to start paying for emissions."

### Explain jargon, don't avoid it
The audience is sustainability/compliance professionals who need to know the terminology. Introduce terms naturally with brief inline explanations rather than avoiding them or over-explaining.

**Do:** "This creates a risk of carbon leakage — when companies shift production to countries with looser environmental rules."
**Don't:** "This creates a risk of something called 'carbon leakage' (which is a technical term meaning...)."

### Cross-reference related topics
Articles frequently reference other regulations, frameworks, or concepts that Coolset covers. This serves both SEO (internal linking) and reader utility.

**Example:** "It complements other EU policies like the EU Taxonomy for sustainable finance and the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)."

### End with actionable next steps
Articles close with practical guidance — what the reader should do now. This is often a short bulleted list of immediate actions.

### Maintain regulatory accuracy
Dates, thresholds, phase-in schedules, and legal requirements must be precise. When regulations are pending or uncertain, this is stated explicitly rather than glossed over.

**Example:** "The revised ESRS published by EFRAG in November 2025 awaits formal European Commission adoption (expected summer 2026)."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Lead with the point
The topic sentence of each paragraph states the key takeaway. Supporting detail follows.

### Use em dashes for asides
Em dashes (—) are preferred over parentheses for inline clarifications and asides.

**Example:** "CBAM targets the embedded GHG emissions in certain imported goods — these are the total emissions released during the production of a product."

### Mix sentence lengths
Short declarative sentences for key points. Slightly longer sentences for context and explanation. This rhythm keeps the reader engaged.

**Example:**
"CBAM was formally adopted on 17 August 2023 with a phased rollout. Since October 2023, importers have been required to report emissions data. From 1 January 2026, they'll also need to buy CBAM certificates to cover those emissions, bringing the cost of imports in line with what EU producers already pay under the ETS."

### Minimise passive voice
Active voice is the default. Passive is used sparingly when the actor is genuinely unknown or when the regulation itself is the subject.

**Do:** "Importers must submit one verified report each year."
**Don't:** "One verified report must be submitted each year by importers."

## What to Avoid

- **Marketing fluff** — No "revolutionary," "game-changing," "cutting-edge." Let the content speak for itself.
- **Fear-mongering** — Present compliance as a practical business task, not a looming threat. Urgency is fine; panic is not.
- **Unnecessary hedging** — Don't say "it might be worth considering" when you mean "do this."
- **Filler introductions** — Skip "In today's rapidly evolving regulatory landscape..." and get to the point.
- **Over-explaining basics** — The audience understands business fundamentals. Don't explain what an importer is.
- **Walls of text** — Break up any paragraph longer than 4 sentences.
- **Exclamation marks** — Professional tone; no exclamation marks in body copy.

## Sample Key Takeaways Block

```
### Key Takeaways
- CBAM introduces a carbon price on imported goods, with reporting already required and certificate payments starting from 1 January, 2026.
- Importers must calculate, report, and pay for embedded emissions, working closely with suppliers to gather data aligned with EU standards.
- Coolset simplifies CBAM readiness with tools for supplier engagement, emissions tracking, and cost forecasting.
```

Note: The last bullet typically references how Coolset helps with the topic — a soft product mention, not a hard sell.

## For LLM Prompting

When generating Coolset-style content, instruct the model to:

1. Write in second person ("you"), professional but conversational tone
2. Open with 2–4 key takeaway bullets
3. Structure with clear H2/H3 hierarchy following What → Why → How → Who → Action
4. Use short paragraphs (1–4 sentences), bullet/numbered lists for details
5. Ground every claim in specific dates, thresholds, or regulation references
6. Explain jargon inline with em dashes, don't avoid technical terms
7. End with concrete next steps the reader can take
8. Use British English spelling conventions
9. Avoid marketing language, filler intros, and passive voice
10. Include a soft Coolset mention in the key takeaways (not in body copy)
